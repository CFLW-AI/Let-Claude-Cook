# Blind Spot Check — Standalone Prompt

The simplest and most accessible tool. No jargon, no setup. Just an honest mirror.

---

## The Prompt

```
I want you to help me find my blind spots — not what I'm wrong about, but what I'm not even looking at.

I'm going to describe my area of work and the claims I'm most confident about. I want you to:

1. MISSING DISCIPLINES
What fields have relevant findings about my topic that I probably haven't encountered? Don't just name them — tell me specifically what they've found that would change how I see my own claims.

2. UNASKED QUESTIONS
What questions am I not asking that I should be? Not gotcha questions — genuinely important questions that my framing is causing me to overlook.

3. POPULATION BLIND SPOTS
Who am I probably not thinking about? If my claims are based on studies of university students in wealthy countries, what would change if we looked at different populations, ages, cultures, or economic contexts?

4. TIMESCALE BLIND SPOTS
Am I making claims about the long term based on short-term evidence? Or vice versa? Where does the timescale of my evidence not match the timescale of my claims?

5. THE UNCOMFORTABLE QUESTION
Ask me the single question about my own work that I probably least want to hear. Not to be adversarial — because it's the question that would most improve my understanding if I sat with it honestly.

My area of work: [DESCRIBE YOUR FIELD/FOCUS]

My most confident claims:
- [CLAIM 1]
- [CLAIM 2]
- [CLAIM 3]
```

---

## Why This Works

Most people, when they ask for feedback, get one of two things: validation or correction. "You're right" or "you're wrong." Neither is as useful as the third option: **"Here's what you're not seeing."**

The Blind Spot Check is designed to provide that third option. It doesn't evaluate your claims — it maps the space *around* your claims that you haven't explored.

---

## Tips for Getting the Most Out of It

- **Be honest about your most confident claims.** The ones you're most certain about are the ones most likely to have unexamined assumptions underneath them. Certainty is where blind spots hide.

- **Don't defend yourself against the results.** The natural response to "here's what you're not seeing" is to explain why you don't need to see it. Resist that. Sit with the discomfort. That's the feeling of a blind spot becoming visible.

- **Pick one blind spot and investigate it.** You don't need to address all of them. Pick the one that bothers you most and spend 30 minutes exploring it. Read one paper from the field you were missing. Think through the unasked question. That's more valuable than reading ten papers in your own field.

- **Run it again after a major project.** Your blind spots shift as your work evolves. The gaps you had last year might be filled — but new ones have probably opened up in places you aren't watching.
